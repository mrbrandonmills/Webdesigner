### Question 1: "What does it mean when you dream about falling?"

Falling dreams are among the most common dream experiences, and they typically relate to feelings of losing control or anxiety about something in your waking life.

**Common Interpretations:**

**1. Loss of Control**
The most frequent meaning. If you're experiencing a situation where you feel things are slipping away - a relationship, job stability, financial security - falling dreams often appear. Your unconscious is processing that anxiety through the physical sensation of falling.

**2. Overwhelm**
Taking on too much? The dream may represent feeling like you're "in over your head" and fear of failing or not meeting expectations.

**3. Need to Let Go**
Interestingly, falling can also be about the need to surrender control. Sometimes we grip too tightly to outcomes, and the dream is showing us what we fear - loss of control - so we can examine whether that fear is serving us.

**4. Life Transitions**
Major changes - new job, moving, ending relationships - can trigger falling dreams. The ground you knew is shifting.

**Context Matters:**

- **Where are you falling from?** A building might relate to career; a cliff to a major life decision.
- **How do you feel?** Terror versus peaceful acceptance changes the interpretation.
- **Do you land?** Landing safely suggests you'll navigate the situation; not landing can mean unresolved anxiety.

**Jungian Perspective:**

Jung would say falling dreams often appear when the ego is too inflated - when we're too identified with our conscious self-image. The fall represents the unconscious correcting that imbalance, reminding us we're not as in control as we think.

**What to Do:**

Ask yourself what situation in life feels unstable or out of control. The dream is highlighting what needs attention.

### Question 2: "Why do I keep having the same dream?"

Recurring dreams are your unconscious mind's way of getting your attention. It keeps sending the same message because the issue hasn't been resolved.

**Why Dreams Recur:**

**1. Unprocessed Emotions**
The most common cause. You're carrying unresolved feelings - grief, anger, anxiety - that need processing. The dream will recur until the emotion is acknowledged and worked through.

**2. Ongoing Life Situations**
If the underlying situation hasn't changed, the dream continues. Stuck in an unfulfilling job? The dream keeps coming until you address it.

**3. Unlearned Lessons**
Sometimes recurring dreams represent patterns you haven't recognized yet. The unconscious keeps highlighting the pattern until you see it.

**4. Trauma**
Traumatic experiences can create recurring dreams as the mind attempts to process what happened. These may need professional support to resolve.

**How to Stop Recurring Dreams:**

**Step 1: Record the Dream in Detail**
Write down exactly what happens, how you feel, what symbols appear. Look for what your unconscious is trying to communicate.

**Step 2: Identify the Message**
What life situation does the dream parallel? What emotion is it expressing? What might it be asking you to do?

**Step 3: Take Action in Waking Life**
Address whatever the dream is pointing to. Have the difficult conversation. Make the change. Process the emotion.

**Step 4: Talk to the Dream**
Before sleep, tell the dream you've received its message and describe what you're doing about it. This sounds strange, but it often works - the unconscious feels heard.

**Example:**

I had a recurring dream about missing trains for years. Once I recognized it was about missed opportunities from playing it too safe, and I started taking more risks in life, the dream stopped.

**When to Seek Help:**

If the recurring dream involves trauma or is significantly disrupting your sleep, consider working with a therapist who specializes in dream work.

### Question 3: "What does water symbolize in dreams?"

Water is one of the most significant dream symbols and almost always relates to emotions and the unconscious mind.

**General Meaning:**

Water represents your emotional state and the depths of your unconscious. The condition and behavior of the water tells you about your inner life.

**Types of Water and Their Meanings:**

**Clear, Calm Water**
Emotional clarity. Peace. Things are flowing well in your inner life. You're in touch with your feelings and they're not overwhelming you.

**Murky or Dirty Water**
Confusion. Unclear emotions. You may not understand what you're feeling or why. Can also indicate contaminated emotions - like resentment that's poisoning other feelings.

**Flooding**
Overwhelming emotions. You're being inundated by feelings - could be grief, anxiety, or any strong emotion you're not fully processing. The unconscious is overflowing into consciousness.

**Drowning**
Feeling consumed by emotional situations. Often appears when you're dealing with more than you can handle emotionally, or when repressed emotions are threatening to overwhelm you.

**Ocean**
The collective unconscious. Vast depths. Can represent feeling small in the face of something much larger than yourself. Also spiritual depths and mystery.

**Rain**
Cleansing, renewal. Emotions washing over you in a purifying way. Can be sadness that's being released.

**Tsunami or Tidal Wave**
Massive emotional upheaval coming. Something overwhelming is approaching in your life - could be external events or internal emotions you've been suppressing.

**Swimming**
How you're navigating emotions. Swimming easily = managing feelings well. Struggling = difficulty with emotional situations.

**Stagnant Water**
Stuck emotions. Feelings that aren't flowing or being expressed. Emotional stagnation.

**Questions to Ask:**

- What is my relationship with the water in the dream?
- Am I in control or is the water controlling me?
- What's the emotional quality of the water?
- What situation in my life involves similar emotional dynamics?

**Jungian View:**

Jung saw water as representing the unconscious itself - that vast realm beneath consciousness. Dreams of water often indicate communication from these depths demanding attention.

### Question 4: "What are nightmares trying to tell us?"

Nightmares are not punishments or random neural firing - they're urgent messages from your unconscious mind. They use fear to ensure you pay attention.

**Why We Have Nightmares:**

**1. Processing Difficult Emotions**
The primary function. Nightmares help process fear, anxiety, grief, or anger that hasn't been dealt with consciously. They're unpleasant but actually helpful - they're doing necessary emotional work.

**2. Warning Signals**
Nightmares can alert you to dangers you're not consciously acknowledging. A job that's harming you, a relationship that's toxic, health you're neglecting - the unconscious sees what you won't look at.

**3. Shadow Material**
Jung's concept of the shadow - the parts of yourself you've rejected - often appears in nightmares. Being chased by monsters? That monster might represent your own repressed aggression, sexuality, or power.

**4. Trauma Processing**
After traumatic experiences, nightmares are the psyche's attempt to process what happened. This is normal and typically decreases over time, though severe trauma may need professional support.

**5. Current Stress**
High stress often triggers nightmares. The unconscious is processing anxieties your conscious mind is managing during the day.

**Common Nightmare Meanings:**

- **Being chased**: Avoiding something in waking life - an emotion, responsibility, or aspect of yourself
- **Falling**: Loss of control, overwhelm, anxiety about failure
- **Death**: Major transition or change (rarely literal)
- **Teeth falling out**: Anxiety about appearance, powerlessness, communication issues
- **Being trapped**: Feeling stuck in a life situation

**How to Work with Nightmares:**

**Step 1: Record Immediately**
Write the nightmare down. Don't avoid it - the avoidance is often what makes it recur.

**Step 2: Identify the Core Emotion**
What were you feeling in the nightmare? That emotion is the message.

**Step 3: Connect to Waking Life**
Where in your life do you feel that emotion? What situation parallels the nightmare?

**Step 4: Take Action**
Address whatever the nightmare is pointing to. The nightmare is urgent because something needs attention.

**Step 5: Talk to the Nightmare**
Before sleep, acknowledge the nightmare's message and state what you're doing about it.

Nightmares typically reduce once their message is received and acted upon.

### Question 5: "How can I remember my dreams better?"

Dream recall is a skill that can be developed. Most people go from "I never dream" to vivid recall within 2-3 weeks using these techniques.

**Why We Forget Dreams:**

You dream multiple times every night - we all do. The issue is memory consolidation. Dreams aren't tagged as "important" by the brain, so they're not retained. But you can change this.

**Techniques That Work:**

**1. Keep a Dream Journal Bedside (Essential)**

This is non-negotiable for dream recall. Keep a physical notebook (not phone - the light disrupts recall) and pen right next to your bed.

Write immediately upon waking. Even fragments. "Something about water. Fear. Someone familiar." Don't wait until you're fully awake - dreams evaporate within minutes.

**2. Set an Intention Before Sleep**

Every night as you're falling asleep, tell yourself: "I will remember my dreams."

This simple act signals to your brain that dream memories matter. It sounds too easy, but it works - intention shapes what the brain retains.

**3. Don't Move Upon Waking**

This is the game-changer most people miss. When you wake up, don't move. Don't open your eyes. Stay in the exact position you woke in.

Dreams are stored in state-dependent memory. Moving your body disrupts access. Lie still and let dream images surface, then write.

**4. Wake Naturally When Possible**

Alarms shock you out of sleep and scatter dream memories. On days when you can wake naturally, recall is dramatically better.

If you must use an alarm, set it for a few minutes before you need to get up. Give yourself transition time.

**5. Review What You Wrote**

After writing, close your eyes and spend 60 seconds mentally reviewing the dream. This strengthens the memory trace.

**6. Look for Patterns**

Weekly, review your dreams for recurring symbols, themes, or emotions. This practice deepens your attention to dreams, which improves recall.

**Troubleshooting:**

- **Still not remembering?** Try setting intention MORE intensely. Visualize yourself waking up and writing.
- **Remembering fragments only?** That's normal at first. Write the fragments. Full dreams will come.
- **Alarm dependency?** Try weekends first until you've built the habit.

**Timeline:**

- Week 1: Fragments, maybe one partial dream per night
- Week 2: Full dreams every other night
- Week 3+: Regular vivid recall

### Question 6: "What are the Jungian archetypes?"

Archetypes are universal patterns of behavior, personality, and motivation that exist in the collective unconscious - the shared psychological inheritance of all humans.

Carl Jung discovered that the same symbolic figures appear across all cultures, myths, religions, and dreams. He called these archetypes.

**The Major Archetypes:**

**The Self**
The archetype of wholeness and the goal of psychological development (individuation). It represents the unity of conscious and unconscious and is often symbolized as mandalas, divine children, or circles.

**The Shadow**
Contains everything about ourselves we've repressed, denied, or never developed. It holds both negative traits we consider unacceptable AND positive traits we've rejected. Appears in dreams as same-sex antagonists or threatening figures.

**The Anima/Animus**
The unconscious feminine side of a man (anima) or masculine side of a woman (animus). Represents the qualities of the opposite gender that we need to integrate for wholeness. Appears as opposite-sex figures in dreams.

**The Persona**
The mask we wear in public - our social identity. Necessary for functioning in society but problematic when we over-identify with it and forget it's not our true self.

**Archetypal Patterns:**

Beyond these major archetypes, Jung and subsequent theorists identified archetypal patterns that shape personality and life direction:

- **The Hero** - Conquers challenges, proves worth through courage
- **The Sage** - Seeks truth and wisdom, understands the world
- **The Explorer** - Seeks freedom, discovers new things
- **The Ruler** - Creates order, takes responsibility
- **The Creator** - Makes new things, expresses imagination
- **The Caregiver** - Nurtures others, provides support
- **The Rebel** - Breaks rules, challenges status quo
- **The Magician** - Transforms reality, manifests vision
- **The Innocent** - Seeks happiness, sees good in everything
- **The Lover** - Pursues connection, appreciates beauty
- **The Jester** - Lives in the moment, uses humor
- **The Everyman** - Connects with others, belongs

**Why Archetypes Matter:**

Understanding your dominant archetypes explains:
- What motivates you
- Why you make certain choices
- Where your strengths and blind spots are
- What kind of work and relationships suit you
- What shadow material you're likely to have

Most people have 2-3 primary archetypes and several secondary ones. Knowing your pattern gives you a map of your psyche.

**How to Identify Your Archetypes:**

- Notice what stories and characters you're drawn to
- Examine your life choices and what drives them
- Look at what you judge in others (often rejected archetypes)
- Analyze your dreams for archetypal figures
- Take an archetypal assessment

### Question 7: "How do I find my purpose in life?"

This is one of the most important questions we can ask, and while I can't give you YOUR answer, I can share frameworks that help people find it.

**Framework 1: The Intersection Model**

Purpose often exists at the intersection of:
- **What you're good at** (skills and talents)
- **What you love doing** (passion and interest)
- **What the world needs** (contribution and impact)
- **What you can be paid for** (sustainability)

Map each circle. Where they overlap is a strong candidate for purpose.

**Framework 2: Archetypal Alignment**

Your purpose is usually aligned with your core archetypal patterns.

- **Sages** find purpose in seeking and sharing wisdom
- **Caregivers** find purpose in nurturing and supporting
- **Creators** find purpose in making and expressing
- **Heroes** find purpose in overcoming challenges
- **Explorers** find purpose in discovery and freedom

When you're living contrary to your archetypes, you feel empty even when "successful." Identifying your patterns clarifies what kind of purpose will fulfill you.

**Framework 3: Follow Meaning, Not Happiness**

Purpose isn't about constant happiness - it's about meaning. Meaningful activities are often challenging, even uncomfortable. But they make you feel alive and significant.

Ask: What makes me feel most alive, even if it's hard?

**Framework 4: Notice What You Can't NOT Do**

What do you keep returning to even when you try to quit? What would you do even if you weren't paid? What do you care about so much it sometimes hurts?

That's usually close to your purpose.

**Framework 5: Your Pain Points to Your Purpose**

What have you struggled with that you've overcome or learned to manage? Your wounds often become your greatest gifts. People who've overcome addiction help addicts. People who've healed trauma help others heal.

3. **Keep a "meaning journal"** - Record moments that feel significant and look for patterns.

### Question 8: "What is the shadow self in psychology?"

The shadow is a concept from Carl Jung's analytical psychology. It refers to the unconscious part of our personality that our conscious ego doesn't identify with.

**What's in the Shadow:**

The shadow contains:

1. **Traits we find unacceptable**
Qualities we've been taught are "bad" - aggression, selfishness, sexuality, weakness - get repressed into the shadow.

2. **Undeveloped potentials**
Positive traits we never developed - creativity, assertiveness, playfulness - also live in the shadow.

3. **Childhood adaptations**
Parts of ourselves we had to suppress to fit in with family or society.

**How the Shadow Forms:**

Throughout childhood, we learn which parts of ourselves are acceptable and which aren't. Praised for being quiet? The loud, expressive part goes into shadow. Punished for anger? It gets repressed. Told you're "not artistic"? The creative capacity goes underground.

The shadow isn't evil - it's just everything that didn't fit our self-image.

**How to Recognize Your Shadow:**

**Projection**
The clearest sign. When you have a strong negative reaction to someone, you're often seeing your shadow projected onto them. "I can't stand how arrogant he is" might mean you've repressed your own confidence.

**Dreams**
Shadow figures appear as same-sex characters who antagonize or frighten you. They show you what you've disowned.

**Slips and "Jokes"**
"Just kidding" often reveals shadow material. So do Freudian slips - the unconscious breaking through.

**Repetitive Problems**
The same issue appearing across different contexts often indicates shadow material at work.

**Why Shadow Work Matters:**

1. **Energy drain** - Keeping the shadow repressed takes enormous energy.
2. **Projection** - We see our shadow in others and react to it, harming relationships.
3. **Limitation** - Repressed traits can't be used, even when needed.
4. **Sabotage** - The shadow acts out in destructive ways when ignored.

**How to Work with the Shadow:**

1. **Notice projections** - When triggered by others, ask what you might share with them.
2. **Analyze dreams** - Pay attention to antagonistic figures.
3. **Write dialogues** - Communicate with shadow parts through journaling.
4. **Consider opposites** - If you identify as patient, examine your impatience.
5. **Ask trusted others** - What do they see in you that you don't?

Integration doesn't mean acting out shadow material - it means acknowledging it and finding healthy expression.

### Question 10: "What personality framework is most accurate?"

This is a great question, and the honest answer is: it depends on what you're trying to understand.

Different frameworks excel at different things. Here's a breakdown:

**For Scientific Validity: Big Five (OCEAN)**

The most empirically validated framework. Measures:
- **O**penness to experience
- **C**onscientiousness
- **E**xtraversion
- **A**greeableness
- **N**euroticism

Pros: Backed by decades of research. Predicts real-world outcomes.
Cons: Descriptive rather than explanatory. Tells you WHAT you are but not WHY.

**For Motivation and Blind Spots: Enneagram**

Identifies 9 core types based on fundamental fears and desires.

Pros: Gets at deep motivation. Excellent for shadow work.
Cons: Less scientific validation. Types can feel limiting.

**For Cognitive Style: Myers-Briggs (MBTI)**

Based (loosely) on Jung's work. 16 types based on preferences in four areas.

Pros: Easy to understand. Good for communication style.
Cons: Limited scientific validity. Binary categories oversimplify.

**For Life Direction and Purpose: Jungian Archetypes**

Identifies which universal patterns (Hero, Sage, Creator, etc.) drive you.

Pros: Connects to deeper meaning. Explains major life choices.
Cons: Less structured. Can require interpretation.

**My Recommendation:**

Use multiple frameworks. They're different lenses on the same reality:

1. **Big Five** for scientific baseline
2. **Archetypes** for motivation and life direction
3. **Enneagram** for fear/desire patterns and shadow work
4. **MBTI** for communication and cognitive style

No single framework captures human complexity. But together they create a richer picture.

**The Real Key:**

Whatever framework you use, the point is self-understanding that leads to better decisions and relationships. The "most accurate" framework is the one that helps you see yourself more clearly and live more effectively.

Also: don't over-identify with any type. You're more complex than any category. Frameworks are maps, not territory.

### Question 11: "What does recurring dream about being chased mean?"

Being chased is one of the most common dream themes, and it typically indicates avoidance - there's something in your waking life you're running from.

**The Core Message:**

What matters isn't what's chasing you (monster, person, unknown threat). What matters is that you're running. The dream is showing you a pattern of avoidance.

**What Are You Avoiding?**

**An Emotion**
Fear, grief, anger - emotions you're not allowing yourself to feel. They keep chasing you because they need to be acknowledged.

**A Responsibility**
A difficult conversation. A decision you're putting off. A task you're dreading.

**An Aspect of Yourself**
Often, the pursuer is your shadow - the part of yourself you've rejected. Running from your own aggression, sexuality, ambition, or vulnerability.

**A Situation**
A job that isn't working. A relationship that's unhealthy. A life path that's wrong for you.

**What's Chasing You:**

While the act of running is most significant, what's chasing you provides clues:

- **Unknown figure or monster**: Often the shadow or repressed emotions
- **Known person**: Qualities of that person you're avoiding in yourself
- **Animal**: Instincts or drives you've repressed
- **Authority figure**: Rules, expectations, or responsibilities
- **Natural disaster**: Overwhelming emotions or life changes

**How to Stop the Dream:**

The dream usually stops when you address what you're avoiding.

1. **Identify the avoidance** - What in life are you running from?
2. **Face it** - Have the conversation, make the decision, feel the emotion
3. **Try "turning around" in the dream** - In lucid dreaming or active imagination, face the pursuer and ask what it wants

Often what's chasing you just wants acknowledgment. Once seen, it stops pursuing.

**Example:**

I had recurring chase dreams during a period when I was avoiding confrontation at work. Once I addressed the conflict directly, the dreams stopped.

**Shadow Work Connection:**

If you're repeatedly chased by a threatening figure, consider shadow work. What have you rejected about yourself that's demanding attention?

### Question 12: "How can I interpret my own dreams?"

Dream interpretation is a skill that develops with practice. Here's a practical framework to get started.

**Step 1: Record the Dream**

Write it down immediately upon waking, before your feet hit the floor. Dreams evaporate within minutes.

Include:
- The narrative/plot
- Characters present
- Settings
- Objects that stood out
- Emotions felt during the dream
- Emotions upon waking

**Step 2: Identify the Feeling**

The emotional content is often more important than the visual content. How did you feel in the dream? That emotion is a key to meaning.

**Step 3: Make Associations**

For each significant symbol, ask:
- What does this mean to ME personally?
- What do I associate with this?
- When have I felt this way?

Personal associations trump universal meanings. If dogs comforted you as a child, a dog in your dream means something different than for someone who was bitten.

**Step 4: Look for Connections to Waking Life**

Ask:
- What current life situation feels like this dream?
- Where in my life am I experiencing these emotions?
- What recent event might have triggered this?

Dreams typically comment on current situations.

**Step 5: Consider Universal Symbols**

While personal associations come first, some symbols have common meanings:
- Water = emotions
- Houses = self/psyche
- Vehicles = how you're moving through life
- Death = transformation/change
- Falling = loss of control

**Step 6: Look for the Compensation**

Jung observed that dreams often compensate for one-sided conscious attitudes. If you're being overly aggressive in life, you might dream of peace. If you're too passive, you might dream of confrontation.

Ask: What balance is this dream trying to restore?

**Step 7: Find the Message**

Dreams want to help. They're not tormenting you randomly. Ask: What is this dream trying to tell me? What does it want me to see or do?

**Common Mistakes:**

- Taking dreams too literally (they speak symbolically)
- Using generic dream dictionaries instead of personal associations
- Ignoring the emotional content
- Overthinking instead of feeling

**Building the Skill:**

Keep a dream journal for at least a month. Review weekly for patterns. Over time, you'll learn your personal symbol language.

### Question 13: "Why do we dream about people we know?"

Dreaming about people you know is very common, but here's the key insight: usually, they're not really about those people. They're about parts of yourself.

**People as Aspects of Self:**

In Jung's view, every person in your dream represents an aspect of your own psyche. When you dream about your mother, you're not really dreaming about her - you're dreaming about what she represents to you internally.

**How to Interpret:**

Ask: What qualities does this person represent to me?

Your critical boss = your own inner critic
Your nurturing grandmother = your nurturing capacity
Your adventurous friend = your unexpressed desire for adventure

The dream is using these people as symbols for parts of yourself.

**Same-Sex Figures:**

Often represent your shadow - the parts of yourself you've rejected or haven't developed.

A same-sex dream figure who's aggressive? Probably your repressed aggression.
One who's confident? Possibly your disowned confidence.

**Opposite-Sex Figures:**

Often represent your anima (if you're male) or animus (if you're female) - the unconscious opposite-gender qualities within you.

A mysterious woman in a man's dream might represent his relationship with intuition, emotion, or creativity.

**When It IS About Them:**

Sometimes, though less often than we think, dreams about people ARE actually about our relationship with them. Signs this might be the case:

- You're actively processing relationship issues with them
- They've recently done something significant
- The dream shows realistic, not symbolic, interactions

**Recurring People:**

If someone keeps appearing in your dreams, they represent something important that needs attention:
- A quality you need to develop
- A shadow aspect to integrate
- An unresolved relationship issue

**Example:**

I kept dreaming about a confident former colleague. I thought it was about missing her. Actually, it was about my own suppressed confidence - she represented a quality I needed to own.

**The Exercise:**

For any person in your dream, list three qualities they represent to you. Then ask: How are those qualities relevant to my life right now?

**Framework 5: Ask Better Questions**

Instead of "What should I do?", ask:

- What would I regret NOT doing?
- What would I do if I knew I couldn't fail?
- What matters more than my comfort?

Your path will likely evolve. What fulfills you at 25 may differ from 45. Stay open and keep listening to yourself.

### Question 15: "What is individuation in Jungian psychology?"

Individuation is Carl Jung's term for the process of psychological development - becoming who you truly are by integrating all parts of your psyche.

**The Basic Concept:**

Individuation is the process of differentiating the self from the collective and integrating unconscious contents into consciousness. It's about becoming a whole, unique individual rather than an unconscious product of social conditioning.

Jung saw it as the purpose of human life in the second half - not success or achievement, but wholeness.

**What Gets Integrated:**

**The Shadow**
All the traits you've repressed or denied - both negative qualities deemed unacceptable and positive qualities you never developed. Integration means acknowledging these parts without necessarily acting them out.

**The Anima/Animus**
The unconscious opposite-gender qualities within you. For men, the anima represents feeling, intuition, relationship. For women, the animus represents logos, assertiveness, analytical ability. Integration means developing these qualities consciously.

**The Persona**
Your social mask. Individuation requires realizing the persona isn't your true self - you wear it, but you're not it.

**The Self**
The archetype of wholeness - the totality of conscious and unconscious. Individuation means organizing the psyche around the Self rather than the ego.

**What Individuation Looks Like:**

- Recognizing and integrating shadow material
- Withdrawing projections
- Developing underdeveloped capacities
- Becoming less rigidly identified with any one part of yourself
- Increasing self-knowledge and authenticity
- Greater tolerance for ambiguity and paradox
- Connection to meaning and purpose

**Common Misconceptions:**

**It's not perfection** - It's wholeness, which includes imperfection.

**It's not individualism** - It actually improves relationships by withdrawing projections.

**It's not a destination** - It's an ongoing process that never fully completes.

**How to Pursue Individuation:**

1. **Shadow work** - Examine what you reject in yourself
2. **Dream analysis** - Let the unconscious communicate
3. **Active imagination** - Dialogue with inner figures
4. **Study your projections** - What you judge in others reveals your shadow
5. **Develop underdeveloped functions** - If you're all thinking, develop feeling
6. **Find meaning** - Connect to something larger than ego

**Signs of Progress:**

- Less triggered by others (projections withdrawn)
- More comfortable with all parts of yourself
- Increased energy (less spent on repression)
- More authentic relationships
- Sense of meaning and direction
